### **Abstract** 

This systematic literature review explores recent technological advancements in the Indian accounting and FinTech sectors, with a focus on the development and application of Domain-Specific Languages (DSLs) and frameworks. The review aims to identify existing research gaps and the need for innovative solutions tailored to the unique requirements of the Indian financial industry. By examining the current state of DSLs in accounting and FinTech, and evaluating their adaptability to the Indian context, this study highlights the potential of developing an Indian DSL, such as eTamil, specifically designed for accounts and FinTech operations. The findings underscore the necessity for targeted research and development to bridge the identified gaps, ultimately contributing to the advancement of the Indian financial ecosystem.

### **3. Results**

#### **3.1 Background and Terminology**

To provide context for this literature review, it's essential to establish a clear understanding of the key concepts and terminology used in the fields of Domain-Specific Languages (DSLs), FinTech, and the Indian financial system.

- **Domain-Specific Languages (DSLs)**:
  - DSLs are specialized programming languages designed for a specific application domain, such as finance or accounting. Unlike general-purpose programming languages, DSLs offer tailored syntax and features that simplify complex tasks within their target domain. In FinTech, DSLs are used to streamline financial operations, enhance accuracy, and reduce development time by providing domain-specific abstractions.

- **FinTech**:
  - FinTech (Financial Technology) refers to the integration of technology into financial services, enabling innovations in banking, insurance, payments, and investment management. The Indian FinTech industry has rapidly expanded, driven by a large unbanked population, increasing smartphone penetration, and government initiatives like Digital India. However, the sector faces unique challenges, including regulatory compliance, cybersecurity concerns, and the need for linguistic and cultural adaptation.

- **Indian Financial Systems**:
  - The Indian financial system encompasses a wide range of institutions, including banks, insurance companies, non-banking financial companies (NBFCs), and payment service providers. The system is regulated by entities like the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) and the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI). Given India's linguistic diversity, financial systems must cater to users across multiple languages, creating a need for technologies that support regional languages.

#### **3.2 Thematic Analysis of the Articles Selected**

The selected articles were analyzed based on their focus areas, resulting in the identification of several key themes relevant to the development of DSLs and frameworks for Indian accounting and FinTech systems.

##### **3.2.1 DSLs in Accounts and FinTech**

- **Global Overview**:
  - DSLs have been successfully implemented in various financial systems worldwide, offering advantages such as reduced development time, improved code readability, and enhanced accuracy in financial operations. Notable examples include DSLs for financial contracts, such as Actus, and domain-specific frameworks like Quantlib.

- **Applicability to the Indian Context**:
  - While these DSLs demonstrate significant benefits, their applicability to the Indian financial sector is limited. Issues such as regulatory differences, the need for multi-language support, and the complexity of Indian financial operations are not fully addressed by existing DSLs. This highlights a gap in the current technology landscape and the need for an Indian-specific DSL.

##### **3.2.2 Frameworks for Accounting and FinTech**

- **Existing Frameworks**:
  - Several frameworks have been developed to support accounting and financial operations, particularly in areas such as risk management, compliance, and transaction processing. Examples include XBRL (eXtensible Business Reporting Language) for financial reporting and Basel II/III frameworks for risk management in banking.

- **Limitations in the Indian Market**:
  - Despite their widespread adoption, these frameworks face challenges when applied to the Indian market. The complexity of Indian regulatory requirements, coupled with the need for financial inclusion across diverse populations, necessitates more adaptable and region-specific frameworks. Additionally, there is a lack of integration with Indian languages, which is crucial for broader accessibility.

##### **3.2.3 Indian Banking, Insurance, and Financial Systems**

- **Current Research**:
  - Research focusing on Indian financial systems has primarily addressed issues such as digital banking, mobile payments, and the impact of blockchain technology. Studies have explored how these innovations can enhance financial inclusion and streamline operations in India’s diverse market.

- **Gaps and Challenges**:
  - Despite these advancements, several challenges remain. For instance, the integration of Indian languages into financial systems is still in its infancy, limiting access for non-English speakers. Moreover, existing research has not fully explored the potential of DSLs in addressing the specific needs of the Indian financial sector, leaving a gap that eTamil aims to fill.

#### **3.3 Meta-analysis of the Articles Selected**

The meta-analysis involved a quantitative and qualitative assessment of the selected literature, with a focus on identifying trends and research gaps in the development of DSLs and frameworks for the Indian financial sector.

- **Quantitative Findings**:
  - A majority of the reviewed articles focused on global DSLs and financial frameworks, with limited research dedicated to the Indian context. This disparity underscores the need for more localized research and development efforts.

- **Qualitative Insights**:
  - The analysis revealed that while there is a growing interest in DSLs and FinTech in India, the integration of these technologies into the Indian financial system remains underexplored. Key areas for further research include the adaptation of DSLs to Indian regulatory environments, the development of frameworks that support multiple languages, and the creation of tools that address the unique challenges of the Indian market.

The review highlighted the development and use of various DSLs in global financial markets. While these DSLs have proven effective in enhancing financial operations, their direct applicability to the Indian market is limited. The primary issues include:

- **Regulatory Challenges**:
  - Global DSLs are often designed with Western regulatory environments in mind, which differ significantly from India's complex and evolving regulatory landscape. Indian regulations demand high levels of customization and flexibility, which existing DSLs may not support.

- **Language Barriers**:
  - The linguistic diversity of India poses a significant challenge. Existing DSLs are generally developed in English and lack the capability to support multiple Indian languages. This limits their usability in a country where a significant portion of the population may not be proficient in English.

- **Cultural and Operational Differences**:
  - Indian financial operations, influenced by unique cultural and economic factors, require DSLs that can handle specific processes like microfinance, rural banking, and informal credit systems, which are less prevalent in other markets.
